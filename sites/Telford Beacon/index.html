Telford Beacon

The original King William IV Dock entrance in Dundee was indicated by the Telford beacon. James Leslie created the design, and it was founded in the middle of the 1830s(in 1834 AD). Thomas Telford, the engineer who had advised Leslie, is credited with naming the lighthouse. It is a lighthouse that once guarded over the entry between King William IV Dock and the tidal harbour. This tiny beacon lighthouse once sat on the wharf between the tidal harbour and King William IV dock to help guide ships in past the various obstacles and hazards in the river. The former harbour light remained in use until the King William IV dock was infilled in the 1960s, as part of the construction of the Tay Road Bridge, specifically the roads connecting to the north end of the bridge.

Built 1834

Telford Beacon was built around 1834 as part of a complex of four large docks in this area of Dundee along with related shipbuilding facilities. Thomas Telford began designing improvements for Dundee harbour in 1815. In 1825 he consulted with James Leslie on the construction of the King William Dock and its associated tidal harbour

1960s

Relocated and became Landlocked after the building of the Tay Road Bridge

In Year 2010

Moved to new Location for a second time. The project involved lifting the entire beacon in one piece and whilst keeping it upright transporting it to its new location overlooking the River Tay.

Present

After additional development of the waterfront region, it is now back to the waterfront where we can see it alongside the bridge.

Thomas Telford

Thomas Telford

Scottish civil engineer Thomas Telford FRS FRSE was born on August 9, 1757, and died on September 2, 1834. Having made a name for himself as an engineer on road and canal projects in Shropshire, he went on to build many infrastructure projects, including tunnels and harbors, in his home country of Scotland.

James Leslie

James Leslie

Scottish civil engineer James Leslie FRSE (25 September 1801, d. 29 December 1889) was an expert in building docks, harbors, bridges, and reservoirs, mostly on Scotland's east coast. In addition, he was a hobby meteorologist.


Dundee Harbour

On the north coast of the Firth of Tay, immediately south of the city center, sits Dundee Harbour. Since the Middle Ages, it has grown closely along with the city's fortunes. This harbor increased in size and significance along with the growth of the whaling, jute, and flax trades, especially in the 19th century. The harbor complex formerly included shipbuilding yards, graving docks, the Victoria Dock and Camperdown Dock, the Earl Grey Dock, the King William IV Dock, and a harbor for the Tay ferry to Newport-on-Tay. At its height, it covered about 48 hectares (119 acres) and provided quayside space extending 3.5 miles (5.6 km).
