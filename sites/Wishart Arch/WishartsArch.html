Wishart's Arch

The Wishart Arch is the name given in more recent times to a remnant of the 16th century city walls of Dundee around a gateway, the East Port or Cowgate. The arch is still situated in a street known as the Cowgate, although this section is isolated from its continuation in the western by the A991 inner ring road. Most likely built when Dundee was fortified around 1590, its survival is due to its probably-mythical connection with the Protestant reformer George Wishart (1513-46), whose is said to have preached from the structure in 1544.

George Wishart

One of the first Protestant martyrs to be burnt at the stake for heresy was the Scottish Protestant reformer George Wishart (also known as Wisehart; c. 1513–1 March 1546). James Wishart's son and Sir John of Pitarrow's brother, George Wishart, identified as Reformer supporters. After graduating from the freshly established University of Aberdeen, he went on a European tour. It is said that he began studying the Reformed beliefs for the first time while he was overseas. He took up teaching Greek at Montrose for a while. After that, Wishart moved to Cambridge, where he lived for almost six years, from 1538 until 1543. Riding in the train of the Commissioners ordered to arrange Prince Edward and the Queen of Scots' marriage, he made his way back to Scotland. He preached to the populace at Montrose, Dundee, and all across Ayrshire, and was well received. Wishart, who had just spoken of being close to death, was captured by Bothwell in the home of Cockburn in Ormiston as he was traveling eastward into the Lothians. After being taken prisoner to St. Andrews, he was tried by a clerical Assembly, declared guilty, and sentenced to death as an uncompromising heretic. The day after, he was crucified at the stake on Castle Green, as his prosecutor, Cardinal David Beaton, also known as Bethune, watched from the castle's windows. Three months later, he would be killed himself.
